\begin{abstract}
Accurate denoising of inertial measurement unit (IMU) signals is a
foundational step for reliable motor symptom assessment in Parkinson's Disease
(PD).
Wrist-worn smartwatches offer an unobtrusive, continuously worn sensing
platform, yet their raw accelerometer and gyroscope recordings are corrupted by
motion artifacts, sensor dropout, ambient vibrations, and electronic noise that
can obscure the clinically critical 4--6\,Hz tremor signature.
In this work we design, implement, and systematically evaluate five denoising
autoencoder architectures---Fully-Connected (FC), 1D-CNN, LSTM, UNet-1D, and a
patch-based Transformer---applied to six-channel wrist IMU recordings from the
PADS Parkinson's Disease Smartwatch Dataset \citep{varghese2023pads}, which
comprises 355 PD patients and healthy controls recorded on Apple Watch Series~4.
Models are trained under four realistic noise regimes (Gaussian, random masking,
impulse, and sinusoidal interference) using a novel hybrid time-frequency
reconstruction loss that combines mean squared error with a spectral $L_1$ term
to explicitly preserve tremor-band power.
We conduct five systematic experiments: architecture comparison, latent-dimension
ablation, noise-level robustness, noise-type cross-evaluation, and
hyperparameter grid search.
Reconstruction quality is assessed via MSE, SNR improvement (SNRi), and a
domain-specific tremor-band power MAE targeting the 4--6\,Hz range.
Empirical results reveal a clear architectural divide governed by a single
structural principle---\emph{skip connections that bypass the information
bottleneck}.
UNet-1D (multi-scale spatial skips) and the patch-based Transformer
(encoder token skips at patch level) both achieve
MSE\,$\approx 0.004$, SNRi\,$\approx +5.4$\,dB, and preserve tremor-band
power MAE to within 0.68\,Hz$^2$ of the clean baseline.
By contrast, FC, CNN, and LSTM architectures---which route all information
through a tight 64-dimensional bottleneck without skip paths---achieve
negative SNRi ($-7$ to $-9$\,dB) and inflate TremorMAE by 94--168$\times$.
A classical Donoho--Johnstone wavelet baseline occupies a middle ground
(SNRi\,$= +0.53$\,dB, TremorMAE 17.69\,Hz$^2$).
These findings demonstrate that skip connections are both necessary and
sufficient for effective denoising of multi-channel PD wrist IMU signals,
with direct clinical implications for tremor monitoring pipelines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Clinical Context and Motivation}

Parkinson's Disease is the second most prevalent neurodegenerative disorder
worldwide, affecting more than 10 million people and projected to double in
prevalence by 2040 as populations age \citep{poewe2017parkinson,dorsey2018global}.
The disease is characterised by a progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in
the substantia nigra, producing the cardinal motor triad of resting tremor,
bradykinesia, and rigidity.
Of these, \emph{resting tremor}---a rhythmic, involuntary oscillation most
prominent at rest and occurring at 4--6\,Hz in the wrist and fingers---is both
the most recognisable symptom and one of the most informative markers for
diagnosis, disease staging, and therapeutic monitoring \citep{deuschl2000tremor,
jankovic2008parkinsons}.

Conventional clinical assessment relies on structured neurological examinations
such as the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS), which are
administered in controlled clinic settings at infrequent intervals.
Such assessments provide only a snapshot of motor function and cannot capture the
substantial within-day fluctuations associated with medication cycles (ON/OFF
states), fatigue, or emotional stress.
This temporal sparsity is a fundamental limitation: patients may present
differently on examination days than in their daily home environment, leading to
under- or over-estimation of symptom burden \citep{maetzler2013quantitative,
bloem2021telehealth}.

Wearable inertial measurement units (IMUs)---particularly those embedded in
consumer-grade smartwatches---offer a compelling alternative.
They record 3-axis accelerometry and gyroscopy at the wrist continuously and
passively during daily life, providing ecologically valid, longitudinal motor
data at scale.
The first author brings direct research experience with Parkinson's Disease
wrist IMU data, having worked extensively with wearable sensor systems for motor
symptom quantification in clinical cohorts, which informs several design
decisions in this work, including the choice of evaluation metrics, the
selection of preprocessing parameters, and the dual-wrist recording strategy.
Several studies have demonstrated the feasibility of extracting clinically
meaningful features from wrist IMU data in PD, including tremor severity
\citep{rigas2012assessment}, gait impairment \citep{salarian2004gait},
postural instability \citep{mancini2011trunk}, and freezing of gait
\citep{camps2018deep}.
Longitudinal remote monitoring further enables tracking of disease
progression and drug efficacy without requiring clinic visits
\citep{patel2009monitoring,lonini2018smartwatch,delrobaei2018ambulatory}.

\subsection{The Signal Quality Problem}

Despite their promise, raw smartwatch IMU recordings are corrupted by multiple
overlapping noise sources.
\emph{Motion artifacts} arise from everyday activities (walking, typing,
gesturing) that superimpose large-amplitude, broadband acceleration signals onto
the subtle tremor oscillations of interest.
\emph{Sensor dropout} occurs when transient contact loss between the watch and
wrist zeros out data segments.
\emph{Vibration artifacts} are introduced by haptic feedback mechanisms in the
watch itself; in the PADS dataset, for example, a notification vibration at task
start corrupts the first 500\,ms of every recording.
\emph{Electronic noise} in the MEMS accelerometer and gyroscope contributes
low-level broadband noise across all frequencies.
\emph{Gravity aliasing} introduces a quasi-static DC offset into the
accelerometer axes that varies with wrist orientation.

These corruptions are particularly damaging for tremor analysis because the
4--6\,Hz clinical tremor band overlaps with low-frequency motion artifacts from
slow deliberate movements.
Classical filtering approaches (e.g., high-pass filtering, wavelet thresholding
\citep{donoho1995wavelet}) apply fixed spectral operations that cannot adapt to
the non-stationary, subject-specific character of the noise.
A learned denoising model that is trained on population-level data has the
potential to capture statistical regularities of both the clean IMU signal
manifold and the noise distribution, enabling more principled and adaptive
artifact removal.

\subsection{Denoising Autoencoders}

Denoising autoencoders (DAEs) \citep{vincent2008extracting,vincent2010stacked}
address this problem directly: given a clean signal $\vx$ and a corrupted
observation $\vx_{\text{noisy}}$, a neural network $f_{\vtheta}$ is trained to
reconstruct $\vx$ from $\vx_{\text{noisy}}$:
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\vx} = f_{\vtheta}(\vx_{\text{noisy}}),
  \qquad
  \vtheta^* = \argmin_{\vtheta}\;
  \E_{\vx,\,\vx_{\text{noisy}}}\bigl[\mathcal{L}(\vx,\,f_{\vtheta}(\vx_{\text{noisy}}))\bigr].
  \label{eq:dae}
\end{equation}
%
Beyond practical denoising, the compressed bottleneck representation
$\vz = \text{Encoder}(\vx_{\text{noisy}}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}$ encodes a
structured latent space of signal dynamics that is potentially useful for
downstream tasks such as tremor severity regression, ON/OFF medication state
classification, or disease staging.
The choice of encoder-decoder architecture determines which aspects of temporal
structure the model can exploit, and different inductive biases may be
better or worse matched to the statistical properties of PD wrist IMU signals.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A complete, reproducible denoising pipeline for 6-channel (dual-axis
    accelerometer and gyroscope) wrist IMU data from the PADS dataset, with
    \emph{subject-level} train/val/test splits that prevent patient-level
    data leakage---a critical requirement for biomedical machine learning.
  \item Implementation and rigorous comparison of five autoencoder architectures
    spanning the FC, 1D-CNN, LSTM, skip-connection (UNet-1D), and
    patch-based Transformer families.
  \item A hybrid time-frequency reconstruction loss combining MSE with a
    spectral $L_1$ term that explicitly incentivises preservation of
    tremor-band power.
  \item Four noise models covering additive Gaussian, random masking, impulse,
    and sinusoidal interference regimes, enabling cross-noise generalisation
    analysis.
  \item A domain-specific evaluation metric---tremor-band power MAE---that
    measures preservation of the 4--6\,Hz frequency content of direct
    clinical relevance in PD.
  \item Five systematic experiments providing actionable insights into the
    relationship between architecture, latent dimension, noise type, and
    clinical signal quality.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Sections~\ref{sec:dataset}--\ref{sec:metrics} describe the dataset,
noise models, architectures, training procedure, and evaluation metrics.
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents and discusses experimental results.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} synthesises findings and limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Wearable Sensing for Parkinson's Disease}

The use of wearable sensors to objectively quantify PD motor symptoms has been
an active research area for two decades.
Early work established the feasibility of detecting PD tremor from wrist
accelerometry using handcrafted spectral features \citep{rigas2012assessment,
tzallas2014unified}.
\citet{salarian2004gait} demonstrated that ambulatory IMU systems could
provide gait metrics (stride length, cadence, gait symmetry) that correlated
with UPDRS scores, while \citet{mancini2011trunk} used trunk accelerometry
to quantify postural instability.
\citet{patel2009monitoring} monitored motor fluctuations in PD patients using
wrist and ankle IMUs over multiple hours, showing that wearables could
capture the cyclic ON/OFF dynamics of levodopa therapy.
More recently, \citet{lonini2018smartwatch} systematically evaluated which
smartwatch sensor streams (accelerometer, gyroscope, heart rate) provided the
most discriminative information for symptom detection, finding that raw IMU
data from the wrist was consistently informative across tasks.
A comprehensive review of quantitative wearable sensing for PD is provided by
\citet{maetzler2013quantitative}, and the potential for telehealth-based remote
monitoring is discussed by \citet{bloem2021telehealth}.

Despite this progress, the vast majority of feature-extraction and
classification pipelines implicitly assume clean or pre-processed signals.
Few works have systematically addressed the signal quality problem as a
dedicated preprocessing step with a learned model.
Our work fills this gap by treating denoising as a first-class supervised
learning problem with clinical evaluation criteria.

\subsection{Denoising Autoencoders}

Denoising autoencoders were introduced by \citet{vincent2008extracting} as a
training strategy to learn robust feature representations: by training an
autoencoder to reconstruct clean inputs from corrupted versions, the encoder
is forced to learn the structure of the data manifold rather than memorising
input-output pairs.
\citet{vincent2010stacked} extended this to deep architectures, showing that
stacked DAEs could pre-train deep networks before supervised fine-tuning.

In the medical imaging domain, \citet{gondara2016medical} applied convolutional
DAEs to MRI denoising, demonstrating that CNN-based autoencoders outperform
classical non-local means filtering.
The natural extension of these ideas to 1D biomedical signals---ECG, EEG,
and IMU---is an active but comparatively under-explored area.
Our work is among the first to apply a comprehensive multi-architecture DAE
comparison to wrist IMU signals in a PD population.

\subsection{Architectures for 1D Signal Processing}

\textbf{Convolutional networks} for 1D signals exploit local temporal patterns
through shared-weight filters that are equivariant to time shifts.
\citet{bai2018tcn} showed that temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) with
dilated causal convolutions match or exceed LSTM performance on many sequence
modelling benchmarks at lower computational cost.

\textbf{Recurrent networks.}
LSTMs \citep{hochreiter1997long} are the standard recurrent architecture for
capturing long-range sequential dependencies.
However, on very long sequences (e.g., $L = 1024$ at 100\,Hz), vanishing
gradients during backpropagation through time remain a practical challenge.
This motivates the architectural modification we introduce in
Section~\ref{sec:lstm}: replacing temporal mean-pooling with final-hidden-state
encoding, which provides a direct gradient path from the latent bottleneck to
the terminal time step.

\textbf{Skip-connection architectures.}
The UNet \citep{ronneberger2015unet} was originally proposed for biomedical
image segmentation and is characterised by skip connections between
encoder and decoder stages.
These connections allow the decoder to access high-resolution encoder features
directly, bypassing the information bottleneck—a property that is especially
valuable when the target output (the clean signal) shares substantial
low-level structure with the input (the noisy signal).
The 1D adaptation of UNet has been applied to audio source separation and ECG
denoising, and we include it here as the strongest prior-art baseline for
waveform-to-waveform mapping.

\textbf{Transformers.}
Self-attention mechanisms \citep{vaswani2017attention} have displaced LSTMs in
natural language processing and, more recently, in time-series modelling
\citep{liu2022time}.
Full self-attention over $L = 1024$ tokens incurs $\mathcal{O}(L^2)$
computational cost; we address this with ViT-style patch tokenisation
\citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}, reducing the effective sequence length to 128
tokens while preserving all temporal information.

\subsection{Signal Denoising for Human Movement Analysis}

Signal denoising has been recognised as a critical preprocessing step in human
movement analysis broadly and in PD research specifically.
Classical approaches include wavelet soft-thresholding \citep{donoho1995wavelet},
which applies frequency-selective shrinkage but uses fixed, non-adaptive bases.
Kalman filtering provides a principled Bayesian approach but requires an explicit
dynamical model of the signal, which is difficult to specify for the complex,
task-dependent motor patterns observed in PD.
\citet{klucken2013unbiased} noted that unprocessed gait metrics from IMU sensors
showed significantly higher variability than pre-filtered versions, emphasising
the practical importance of artifact removal for reliable clinical inference.
The deep learning approach we adopt avoids the need for handcrafted signal models
and learns the denoising transformation directly from data.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{PADS — Parkinson's Disease Smartwatch Dataset}

We use the PADS dataset \citep{varghese2023pads}, publicly available on
PhysioNet \citep{physionet2000}, DOI: \texttt{10.13026/cs4e-1060}.
The dataset contains wrist IMU recordings from 469 subjects collected with
Apple Watch Series~4 at 100\,Hz across six diagnostic groups:
276 with Parkinson's Disease (PD), 79 Healthy Controls (HC), 28 with Essential
Tremor, 60 with Other Movement Disorders, 15 with Atypical Parkinsonism, and
11 with Multiple Sclerosis.
We restrict our cohort to \textbf{PD (276) and HC (79)}, totalling 355
subjects, to maintain a clinically interpretable binary contrast and avoid
confounding symptom profiles from other tremor disorders.

\textbf{Participant demographics.}
Subjects span ages 40--90 (mean $64.2 \pm 10.7$ years), heights $174.2 \pm
9.2$\,cm, and weights $81.9 \pm 15.4$\,kg.
The PD cohort is 195 male / 81 female; the HC cohort is 29 male / 50 female,
reflecting the known male predominance of PD
\citep{poewe2017parkinson}.
At the time of data preparation, 247 subjects (approx. 70\% of the
target cohort) had been fully downloaded, providing 5\,403 timeseries files
covering both wrists.

\subsection{Recording Protocol and Tasks}

Each subject performed \textbf{11 standardised motor tasks} while wearing the
Apple Watch on both wrists simultaneously, providing a within-subject bilateral
comparison:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
\textbf{Task} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
Relaxed & Arms at rest, seated \\
RelaxedTask & At rest while performing a cognitive task \\
CrossArms & Arms folded across the chest \\
StretchHold & Arms outstretched horizontally \\
HoldWeight & Holding 0.5\,kg, arm outstretched \\
LiftHold & Lifting and holding the arm \\
PointFinger & Index finger pointing forward \\
TouchIndex & Alternating finger-to-nose (both hands) \\
TouchNose & Finger-to-nose targeting \\
DrinkGlas & Simulated drinking motion \\
Entrainment & Rhythmic movement following an auditory cue \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Raw recordings are stored per task per wrist as comma-separated files with
7 columns (no header): \texttt{Time, Accel\_X, Accel\_Y, Accel\_Z,
Gyro\_X, Gyro\_Y, Gyro\_Z}.
We discard the timestamp column and retain all \textbf{6 sensor channels} for
both wrists.

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}

\textbf{Vibration artifact removal.}
The first 50 samples ($= 500$\,ms) of every recording contain a smartwatch
haptic notification artifact at task start that corrupts the accelerometer
baseline.
These are discarded unconditionally before any further processing.

\textbf{Fixed-length windowing.}
Each recording is segmented into windows of $L = 1024$ samples
(10.24\,s at 100\,Hz).
The last partial window is zero-padded to $L$ samples if shorter.
During training, overlapping windows with hop size 128 samples (87.5\% overlap)
are also generated as a data augmentation strategy, yielding approximately
$8\times$ more training examples per recording.
Validation and test sets use non-overlapping windows only to prevent evaluation
inflation from correlated samples.

\textbf{Robust CSV parsing.}
Some PADS files contain malformed rows (column-count mismatches) resulting from
recording interruptions during data collection.
We use \texttt{pandas.read\_csv} with \texttt{on\_bad\_lines='skip'} and
subsequently remove any rows containing NaN values.

\textbf{Z-score normalisation.}
Per-channel mean $\mu_c$ and standard deviation $\sigma_c$ are computed over
all windows in the \emph{training set} and applied identically to validation
and test splits:
%
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{x}_{c,t} = \frac{x_{c,t} - \mu_c}{\sigma_c + \epsilon},
  \qquad \epsilon = 10^{-8}.
\end{equation}
%
Computing statistics from the training split only prevents data leakage and
ensures that the normalisation is consistent with a deployable pipeline where
test data is not seen during calibration.

\subsection{Subject-Level Data Split}

To prevent patient-level data leakage---a critical requirement in biomedical
machine learning where models may otherwise memorise patient-specific patterns
rather than generalising across individuals---the train/val/test split is
performed \textbf{by subject}, not by window.
Subject IDs are shuffled with fixed seed 42 and divided 80/10/10:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Split} & \textbf{Subjects} & \textbf{Windows (approx.)} & \textbf{Duration} \\
\midrule
Train & $\approx$197 & $\approx$10\,300 & $\approx$29.3\,h \\
Validation & $\approx$25 & $\approx$520 & $\approx$1.5\,h \\
Test & $\approx$25 & $\approx$520 & $\approx$1.5\,h \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Only subjects with at least one downloaded timeseries file on disk are included,
so splits remain valid as the dataset download progresses.

\section{Noise Models}
\label{sec:noise}

To generate (clean, noisy) training pairs without requiring paired real-world
recordings, we apply four synthetic corruption processes to the preprocessed
signals.
All functions operate on tensors of shape $(B, C, L)$ and are applied
independently at each training step, so the model never sees the same corrupted
version of a window twice during training.
The four noise types span qualitatively distinct corruption regimes commonly
encountered in wrist wearable recordings.

\subsection{Gaussian Noise}

Additive white Gaussian noise:
%
\begin{equation}
  \vx_{\text{noisy}} = \vx + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
  \qquad
  \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\,\sigma^2 \mathbf{I}).
\end{equation}
%
This is the classical noise model that provides a useful baseline and
approximates high-frequency electronic sensor noise.
Default $\sigma = 0.1$ (10\% of the normalised signal amplitude);
the robustness experiment sweeps $\sigma \in \{0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4\}$.

\subsection{Random Masking (Dropout Noise)}

Contiguous signal segments are zeroed out to simulate complete sensor dropout
events (e.g., transient wrist contact loss, Bluetooth transmission failures):
%
\begin{equation}
  x_{\text{noisy}}[b,\,c,\,i:i+L_{\text{mask}}] = 0,
  \quad \text{with probability } p_{\text{mask}} \text{ at each } i.
\end{equation}
%
Parameters: $p_{\text{mask}} = 0.1$, $L_{\text{mask}} = 20$ samples (200\,ms).
Critically, the \textbf{same time-mask is applied across all $C=6$ channels},
simulating a physical sensor dropout (all axes simultaneously) rather than
independent per-channel noise, which is the physically realistic failure mode
for wrist IMUs.

\subsection{Impulse Noise}

Random large-amplitude spikes are inserted to simulate sudden
motion artifacts (e.g., impact events, device adjustment):
%
\begin{equation}
  x_{\text{noisy}}[b,c,t] = x[b,c,t] + A \cdot s[b,c,t] \cdot \mathbf{1}[u[b,c,t] < p_{\text{imp}}],
\end{equation}
%
where $u \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]$, $s \in \{-1,+1\}$ is random sign,
$A = 3.0$, and $p_{\text{imp}} = 0.05$.
At 5\% impulse probability, approximately 51 samples per channel per window are
corrupted.

\subsection{Sinusoidal Interference}

A fixed-frequency sinusoidal tone is added to simulate periodic background
interference (e.g., machinery vibration, electrical 50/60\,Hz components
scaled to the sensor range):
%
\begin{equation}
  \vx_{\text{noisy}} = \vx + A\,\sin(2\pi f L \cdot t),
  \qquad t \in [0,\,1],
\end{equation}
%
with $A = 0.3$ and relative frequency $f = 0.05$ (5\,Hz in the 100\,Hz
system, directly overlapping the PD tremor band).
The interference vector has shape $(L,)$ and broadcasts naturally across the
$(B, C)$ batch and channel dimensions via PyTorch broadcasting semantics.
This noise type is the most challenging clinically because it occupies the
same spectral region as the tremor of interest.

\section{Model Architectures}
\label{sec:models}

All five architectures follow the encoder-decoder paradigm with a
$d_z$-dimensional bottleneck (default $d_z = 64$).
Input and output tensors share shape $(B, 6, 1024)$: batch size, channels
(Accel$_X$, Accel$_Y$, Accel$_Z$, Gyro$_X$, Gyro$_Y$, Gyro$_Z$),
and samples.
The FC and LSTM architectures treat all channels globally or sequentially;
CNN, UNet, and Transformer architectures operate on the channel-first
tensor natively.

\subsection{Fully-Connected Autoencoder (FC)}
\label{sec:fc}

The FC autoencoder serves as the lower-bound baseline: the entire
$6 \times 1024 = 6144$-element window is flattened to a vector before
encoding, discarding all temporal and spatial structure.
Each hidden layer uses ReLU activation followed by Dropout ($p=0.2$)
\citep{srivastava2014dropout}:
%
\begin{equation*}
  \underbrace{6144}_{\text{flatten}} \xrightarrow{\text{Lin+ReLU+Drop}} 256
  \xrightarrow{\text{Lin+ReLU+Drop}} 128
  \xrightarrow{\text{Lin}} d_z
  \xrightarrow{\text{Lin+ReLU+Drop}} 128
  \xrightarrow{\text{Lin+ReLU+Drop}} 256
  \xrightarrow{\text{Lin}} 6144.
\end{equation*}
%
\textbf{Parameters:} 3.23M.
The dropout regularisation is necessary because the model has far more
parameters than training windows at the current dataset scale.

\subsection{1D Convolutional Autoencoder (CNN)}
\label{sec:cnn}

The CNN exploits \emph{local temporal patterns} through strided convolutions
with shared weights across time.
Four encoder stages progressively halve the temporal resolution while
expanding feature depth:
%
\begin{equation*}
  (6,\,1024) \xrightarrow{32,\,k3,\,s2} (32,\,512) \xrightarrow{64}
  (64,\,256) \xrightarrow{128} (128,\,128) \xrightarrow{256}
  (256,\,64) \xrightarrow{\text{FC}} d_z.
\end{equation*}
%
Each Conv1d layer (kernel~3, stride~2, padding~1) is followed by ReLU and
Dropout ($p=0.1$).
The decoder mirrors this with ConvTranspose1d (kernel~4, stride~2, padding~1)
to upsample back to the original length; a final trim or pad corrects for
rounding.
\textbf{Parameters:} 2.42M.

\subsection{LSTM Autoencoder}
\label{sec:lstm}

The LSTM \citep{hochreiter1997long} models \emph{long-range temporal
dependencies} through gated recurrent units.
The signal is transposed to $(B, L, C)$ before encoding.

\textbf{Encoder.}
A 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (hidden size 128) reads the sequence in both
directions.
A key modification distinguishes our implementation from the baseline:
rather than mean-pooling over all 1024 hidden states (which causes severe
vanishing gradients through 1024 unrolled time steps), we concatenate only
the \emph{final} forward and backward hidden states of the top layer,
forming a $(B, 256)$ context vector, then project linearly to $d_z$.
This provides a direct gradient path from the bottleneck to the terminal step
of each direction.

\textbf{Decoder.}
Two separate projections map $\vz$ to the initial hidden state $\mathbf{h}_0$
and cell state $\mathbf{c}_0$ of a 2-layer unidirectional decoder LSTM
(hidden size 128).
Zero-valued inputs of shape $(B, L, C)$ are used as the input sequence,
allowing the LSTM to generate the full reconstruction autoregressively from
its initialised state.
An output linear layer projects from hidden size to $C$ channels.
Dropout ($p=0.2$) is applied between LSTM layers.
\textbf{Parameters:} 787K.

\subsection{1D UNet Autoencoder (UNet-1D)}
\label{sec:unet}

The UNet \citep{ronneberger2015unet} augments the CNN encoder-decoder with
\emph{skip connections} that concatenate encoder feature maps directly into
the corresponding decoder stage (Figure~\ref{fig:unet}).
This creates a short-circuit path allowing the decoder to access fine-grained
local structure without routing it through the bottleneck---particularly
valuable for preserving high-frequency tremor bursts.
The bottleneck still provides a low-dimensional representation $\vz$ of global
signal dynamics; the skip connections handle the fine detail.

Four encoder blocks (Conv1d, stride~2, ReLU) feed a linear FC bottleneck to
$d_z$.
Four decoder blocks (ConvTranspose1d, stride~2, ReLU) each receive the
corresponding encoder output concatenated along the channel axis before
convolution, doubling the input channels at each decoder stage.
A utility function handles length mismatches from strided convolution rounding.
\textbf{Parameters:} 2.46M.

\subsection{Patch-Based Transformer Autoencoder}
\label{sec:transformer}

Full self-attention over $L = 1024$ time steps incurs
$\mathcal{O}(L^2 \cdot d_\text{model})$ memory per layer, which is prohibitive
at batch size 64 on a single GPU.
We adopt a ViT-style \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit} \emph{patch tokenisation}:
the signal is divided into non-overlapping patches of $P = 8$ samples,
yielding $N_P = L/P = 128$ tokens per window.
Each patch $\in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 8 = 48}$ is linearly projected to
$d_\text{model} = 128$, reducing attention complexity to
$\mathcal{O}(N_P^2) = \mathcal{O}(128^2)$---64$\times$ smaller than
full-sequence attention---while preserving all temporal content.

\textbf{Encoder.}
Learnable positional embeddings of shape $(1, 128, 128)$ are added to the
projected tokens.
Two Pre-LayerNorm Transformer encoder layers ($n_\text{heads} = 4$,
$d_\text{ff} = 256$, dropout 0.1) \citep{vaswani2017attention} process
the token sequence.
Mean pooling over the patch dimension followed by a linear layer gives $\vz$.

\textbf{Decoder.}
The latent vector is projected back to $d_\text{model}$ and tiled to
$(B, 128, d_\text{model})$.
Positional embeddings are added and two further Transformer encoder layers
(same configuration) refine the sequence.
Each token is decoded to a patch vector via a linear layer;
all patches are reassembled to $(B, 6, 1024)$.

The Pre-LN variant \citep{vaswani2017attention} (normalisation before attention
and feed-forward sublayers) is used throughout for more stable gradient flow.
\textbf{Parameters:} 575K.

\subsection{Classical Wavelet Denoising Baseline}
\label{sec:wavelet}

To contextualise the learned architectures, we include a classical
\emph{Donoho--Johnstone soft-thresholding} baseline \citep{donoho1995wavelet}.
This is the gold-standard non-parametric denoising method for 1D signals and
has been widely used in biomedical signal processing
\citep{reaz2007techniques,klucken2013unbiased}.

The algorithm operates independently on each of the $C = 6$ channels:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0pt]
  \item Decompose the signal using the Daubechies-8 DWT to level $J = 4$,
        yielding one approximation subband $\mathbf{a}_J$ and four detail
        subbands $\{\mathbf{d}_j\}_{j=1}^{J}$.
  \item Estimate the noise standard deviation from the finest-scale detail
        coefficients via the median absolute deviation (MAD) estimator:
        $\hat{\sigma} = \text{median}(|\mathbf{d}_J|) / 0.6745$.
  \item Apply the \emph{universal} soft threshold
        $\lambda = \hat{\sigma}\sqrt{2\ln N}$ to all detail subbands,
        leaving the approximation subband unchanged:
        $\hat{d} = \text{sgn}(d)\,\max(|d| - \lambda,\, 0)$.
  \item Reconstruct with the inverse DWT.
\end{enumerate}

This method requires \emph{no training}: the threshold is set analytically
from each test sample.
It therefore appears in the architecture comparison as a fixed, non-parametric
reference point.
The wavelet baseline is particularly relevant because the PD tremor band
(4--6\,Hz) occupies a specific set of DWT scales: at 100\,Hz sampling with
level-4 Daubechies-8 decomposition, scale~4 covers 3.1--6.25\,Hz, overlapping
almost exactly with the tremor band.
A well-tuned soft-threshold should preserve these coefficients while
suppressing out-of-band noise.
\textbf{Parameters:} 0 (non-trainable).

\section{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Hybrid Time-Frequency Reconstruction Loss}

A purely MSE-minimising objective can achieve low reconstruction error by
over-smoothing transients, which suppresses high-frequency content including
the 4--6\,Hz tremor oscillations of clinical interest.
To counteract this, we use a \emph{hybrid} loss combining time-domain MSE with
a spectral $L_1$ term:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\vtheta)
  = \underbrace{\|\hat{\vx} - \vx\|_2^2}_{\text{MSE}}
  + \;\alpha\;\underbrace{
      \bigl\|\,|\mathcal{F}(\hat{\vx})| - |\mathcal{F}(\vx)|\,\bigr\|_1
    }_{\text{spectral } L_1},
  \qquad \alpha = 0.1,
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{F}$ denotes the real-valued FFT computed along the time axis
with orthonormal normalisation (\texttt{norm="ortho"}).
The spectral term penalises deviations in the power spectrum of the
reconstruction from the clean signal spectrum, providing an explicit gradient
signal for frequency-selective denoising.
The weight $\alpha = 0.1$ was chosen to balance the two terms such that neither
dominates early in training.

An important implication is that the training loss and test MSE are on
\emph{different scales} (the former includes the spectral term); only test MSE
values are compared across architectures.

\subsection{Optimisation}

All models are optimised with Adam \citep{kingma2015adam} (weight decay
$\lambda = 10^{-5}$) for 100 epochs.
A \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} scheduler halves the learning rate when validation
loss does not improve for 10 consecutive epochs (factor 0.5, monitored on
validation hybrid loss).
Gradient clipping (max $L_2$ norm 1.0) stabilises LSTM training during early
epochs when gradients can be large.
The model checkpoint with the lowest validation hybrid loss is retained for
evaluation.

\subsection{Experimental Infrastructure}

Each run is stored in an automatically created timestamped directory
(\texttt{results/YYYYMMDD\_HHMMSS/}) alongside a \texttt{run\_config.json}
snapshot of all hyperparameter values, ensuring complete reproducibility.
Optional \texttt{--tag} annotations allow human-readable run identification
(e.g., \texttt{--tag nodropout} to compare ablations).

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\textbf{Mean Squared Error (MSE).}
$\text{MSE} = N^{-1}\sum_i \|\hat{\vx}_i - \vx_i\|_2^2$, averaged over all
test windows.
MSE provides a global measure of reconstruction fidelity but does not
differentiate between errors in clinically important vs. irrelevant frequency
bands.

\textbf{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR).}
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{SNR}(\vx, \hat{\vx}) = 10\log_{10}
    \frac{\|\vx\|_2^2}{\|\vx - \hat{\vx}\|_2^2}\;[\text{dB}].
\end{equation}
%
SNR normalises reconstruction error by signal energy, making it invariant to
amplitude scaling.
A 3\,dB improvement corresponds to halving the residual noise power.

\textbf{SNR Improvement (SNRi).}
$\text{SNRi} = \text{SNR}(\vx, \hat{\vx}) - \text{SNR}(\vx, \vx_{\text{noisy}})$.
SNRi measures the net benefit of denoising over the raw noisy input.
Positive SNRi indicates that the model successfully reduces noise beyond what is
already present in the input; negative SNRi indicates that the model worsens
the signal.

\textbf{Tremor-Band Power MAE.}
This domain-specific metric is motivated by the clinical importance of the
4--6\,Hz frequency band in PD, where resting tremor manifests:
%
\begin{equation}
  \tremorMAE = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i
    \bigl|\,P_{4\text{--}6}(\hat{\vx}_i) - P_{4\text{--}6}(\vx_i)\,\bigr|,
  \qquad
  P_{4\text{--}6}(\vx) = \!\!\!\sum_{f \in [4,\,6]\,\text{Hz}}\!\!\! |\mathcal{F}(\vx)[f]|^2.
\end{equation}
%
We report both the \emph{input} TremorMAE (noisy signal vs.\ clean) and
\emph{output} TremorMAE (reconstruction vs.\ clean).
A model with low MSE but high TremorMAE is clinically harmful: it achieves
good average reconstruction at the cost of distorting the very spectral
content that carries tremor information.
This metric is only meaningful in the PD context and is novel to our
evaluation framework.

bypass at patch granularity.
Neither model needs to pack all signal detail into 64 numbers.
FC, CNN, and LSTM lack any bypass: every bit of waveform structure must
survive 6144$\to$64 compression, and the resulting information loss exceeds
the noise level, producing negative SNRi.

\textbf{Analysis.}
Three structural patterns emerge from Table~\ref{tab:noise-types}.

\textbf{(i) Impulse noise yields positive SNRi for every model regardless of
training type.}
Even FC trained on sinusoidal noise achieves $+7.4$\,dB on impulse test data.
This is because impulse spikes are extreme outliers; any denoising operation
that reconstructs a smooth signal will remove them by default, regardless of
what noise it was trained to remove.

\textbf{(ii) Skip-connection models achieve large matched-condition SNRi on
specialised noises.}
UNet-1D trained on impulse achieves $+14.0$\,dB; on sinusoidal $+20.5$\,dB.
The Transformer reaches $+16.0$\,dB on impulse and $+30.1$\,dB on sinusoidal
(the sharp spectral peak at 5\,Hz is particularly amenable to spectral
filtering learned by the Transformer's global attention).
However, this specialisation comes at a cost: UNet-1D trained on sinusoidal
drops to $-6.9$\,dB on Gaussian and $-5.7$\,dB on masking;
Transformer trained on sinusoidal gives $-7.3$\,dB on Gaussian.

\textbf{(iii) Gaussian training provides the most robust cross-noise
generalisation.}
UNet-1D trained on Gaussian achieves $+3.8$\,dB (Gaussian), $-0.1$\,dB
(masking), $+3.6$\,dB (impulse), $+0.5$\,dB (sinusoidal) --- all near zero
or positive.
The Transformer shows a similar pattern ($+3.5$, $-0.3$, $+2.1$, $+0.3$\,dB).
This makes Gaussian-trained models the safest choice for real-world deployments
where the noise type is unknown.

The Wavelet baseline is uniformly modest ($-0.1$ to $+0.8$\,dB) and does not
specialise, reflecting the noise-agnostic nature of the MAD threshold.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Inductive Bias and Generalisation}

The experiments reveal that inductive bias alone cannot explain the
$200\times$ MSE gap across architectures.
All five learned models share the same training procedure, loss, and
optimiser; yet their MSE spans 0.004 to 1.542.
The governing factor is not the \emph{type} of inductive bias (translation
equivariance for CNN, sequential order for LSTM) but the
\emph{presence or absence of skip connections}.

FC has no temporal inductive bias and must rediscover temporal locality
entirely from data.
The MLP bottleneck ($6144\to64\to6144$) is too severe at this dataset scale:
every cycle of PD tremor activity and every gyroscope phase relationship must
survive the compression, leading to MSE\,$= 1.509$ and SNRi\,$= -8.96$\,dB.

CNN's translation-equivariant convolutions are well-matched to the
stationarity of IMU noise sources, and the monotonically improving latent
sweep (MSE 1.418 $\to$ 0.826 as $d_z$ grows 8$\to$128; Table~\ref{tab:latent})
confirms that the encoder learns genuinely useful representations.
Without skip connections, however, those representations must survive the
bottleneck intact, capping performance at MSE\,$= 0.831$ and
SNRi\,$= -7.23$\,dB.

LSTM's sequential bias could in principle exploit the phase structure of
4--6\,Hz tremor.
In practice, its flat latent sweep
(MSE 1.895 at $d_z=8$ down to only 1.766 at $d_z=128$;
Table~\ref{tab:latent}) indicates that compressing 1\,024 recurrent steps
into 64 numbers discards phase information regardless of bottleneck width.

\subsection{Skip Connections and Tremor Preservation}

The $200\times$ MSE gap between the two groups (0.004 vs.\ 0.831--1.542)
traces directly to skip connections.
UNet-1D passes encoder feature maps at four spatial resolutions directly to
the decoder, making the denoising task a \emph{residual problem} at each
scale---the decoder needs only to estimate the noise component, not
reconstruct the signal from scratch \citep{he2016deep}.
The Transformer achieves the same effect via a different mechanism: its
decoder adds the full $128$-token encoder patch sequence ($128\!\times\!128$
dimensions) to its input before reconstruction, providing per-patch bypass
of the global latent bottleneck.
Neither model is forced to pack all waveform detail into 64 numbers;
FC, CNN, and LSTM are.

The TremorMAE metric makes the clinical consequence explicit.
FC, CNN, and LSTM amplify the 4--6\,Hz band distortion from 0.53--0.54
to 64--90\,Hz$^2$ (a 118--168$\times$ increase), rendering downstream tremor
severity assessment unreliable.
UNet-1D and Transformer each keep TremorMAE below 0.68\,Hz$^2$, matching
the pre-denoising baseline and preserving the diagnostic signal.
The spectral $L_1$ component of the hybrid loss (Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss})
reinforces this: it provides an explicit gradient signal from the power
spectrum, counteracting the tendency of MSE-only training to suppress
clinically important high-frequency content.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

From a clinical deployment perspective, the findings of this work suggest
several practical guidelines.

\emph{Architecture choice.}
Only UNet-1D and Transformer are viable architectures for wrist IMU denoising
at the current dataset scale.
Both achieve identical fidelity (MSE\,$\approx 0.004$, SNRi\,$\approx +5.4$\,dB,
TremorMAE\,$< 0.68$\,Hz$^2$) across all evaluation metrics.
The Transformer's global self-attention enables higher matched-condition SNRi
on specialised noise types (e.g., $+30.1$\,dB on sinusoidal interference,
Table~\ref{tab:noise-types}), while UNet-1D's spatial skip connections yield
more uniform cross-noise behaviour.
FC, CNN, and LSTM are not suitable for clinical denoising without skip
connections regardless of the hyperparameter configuration.

\emph{Noise model choice.}
Experiment~5 exposes a sharp specialisation--robustness trade-off.
Training on sinusoidal noise yields $+20.5$\,dB (UNet-1D) and $+30.1$\,dB
(Transformer) on the matched test type, but catastrophically transfers:
UNet-1D drops to $-6.9$\,dB and Transformer to $-7.3$\,dB when evaluated on
Gaussian noise.
Gaussian training avoids this pitfall.
UNet-1D trained on Gaussian achieves ($+3.8$, $-0.1$, $+3.6$, $+0.5$)\,dB
across (Gaussian, masking, impulse, sinusoidal) test types; the Transformer
achieves ($+3.5$, $-0.3$, $+2.1$, $+0.3$)\,dB.
All values are near zero or positive.
For deployment where the noise composition is unknown---which is the typical
clinical setting---Gaussian training is the recommended strategy.

\emph{Evaluation metric choice.}
Aggregate MSE is insufficient as a standalone clinical evaluation metric.
A model with low MSE but high TremorMAE may impair downstream tremor analysis
by suppressing the diagnostic signal.
We recommend reporting TremorMAE alongside MSE and SNRi for any denoising
method applied to PD wrist IMU data.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations of the current work should be acknowledged.

\textbf{Synthetic noise assumption.}
All noise is synthetically generated and applied post-hoc to clean signals.
Real-world IMU noise is correlated, non-stationary, and often inseparable
from the motion signal itself.
A model trained on synthetic noise may not generalise perfectly to the
complex artifact patterns observed during actual daily-life recordings.

\textbf{Dataset scale.}
With $\approx$247 of 355 target subjects downloaded at evaluation time, the
validation and test splits contain only $\approx$25 subjects each.
This limits the statistical power of cross-architecture comparisons and may
inflate subject-specific variance in the test metrics.

\textbf{Clean signal ground truth.}
The PADS dataset does not provide paired clean/noisy recordings; ``clean''
signals are the raw recordings assumed to be artifact-free.
In practice, even the raw recordings contain residual motion artifacts.
A gold-standard evaluation would use controlled lab recordings with
simultaneous clean reference measurements.

\textbf{Reduced training budget for ablations.}
The noise-type cross-evaluation (Experiment~5) used 20 epochs per
train$\times$test combination to keep compute tractable; the latent sweep
(Experiment~3) used 50 epochs.
Some cells in Tables~\ref{tab:latent} and~\ref{tab:noise-types} may not
have fully converged, particularly for FC and LSTM, which converge more
slowly.

\textbf{No downstream task evaluation.}
We evaluate reconstruction quality directly (MSE, SNRi, TremorMAE) but do
not assess whether denoising improves a downstream clinical task such as
tremor severity regression or PD vs.\ HC classification.
Such end-task evaluation is the ultimate clinical validation criterion.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a comprehensive evaluation of six denoising architectures
(FC, CNN, LSTM, UNet-1D, patch Transformer, and a Wavelet baseline) for
Parkinson's Disease wrist IMU signals, applied to the PADS dataset of 247
PD patients and healthy controls wearing Apple Watch Series~4.
The study addresses a practically important but under-explored problem:
removing realistic noise from the 6-channel, 100\,Hz wrist signals that carry
information about PD motor symptoms, particularly the 4--6\,Hz tremor
signature.

Key findings and contributions include:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Skip connections are the governing architectural principle.}
    UNet-1D and Transformer both achieve MSE\,$\approx 0.004$ and
    SNRi\,$\approx +5.4$\,dB, a $200\times$ MSE advantage over FC, CNN, and
    LSTM (MSE 0.831--1.542, SNRi $-7$ to $-9$\,dB).
    The two architectures reach the same level via different bypass
    mechanisms: UNet-1D via multi-scale spatial skip connections;
    Transformer via patch-token encoder outputs passed directly into the
    decoder.
    Without a bypass, the 6144$\to$64 bottleneck introduces more distortion
    than the injected noise, producing negative SNRi.
  \item \textbf{Tremor-band preservation separates clinically viable from
    harmful models.}
    FC, CNN, and LSTM inflate TremorMAE from 0.54 to 64--90\,Hz$^2$
    (118--168$\times$), rendering downstream tremor assessment unreliable.
    UNet-1D and Transformer keep TremorMAE below 0.68\,Hz$^2$, matching
    the pre-denoising baseline.
    The hybrid time-frequency loss (MSE + $\alpha \cdot$ spectral $L_1$,
    $\alpha=0.1$) provides the gradient signal needed to achieve this
    spectral fidelity.
  \item \textbf{Gaussian training provides the most robust deployment
    strategy.}
    The noise-type cross-evaluation reveals a sharp specialisation--robustness
    trade-off for skip-connection models: sinusoidal training achieves
    $+20$--$+30$\,dB on the matched type but drops to $-7$\,dB on Gaussian.
    Gaussian-trained UNet-1D and Transformer remain near-zero or positive
    across all four noise types, making them the safest choice when the
    in-deployment noise composition is unknown.
  \item \textbf{Noise-type generalisation is architecture-dependent, and
    specialisation carries cross-noise risk.}
    Cross-noise evaluation reveals that no single model generalises equally
    well across all noise types when trained on a single type.
    Mixed-noise training strategies (sampling the noise type per batch or per
    epoch) are a promising direction for future work.
\end{enumerate}

This work provides a foundation for integrating learned signal denoising
into wearable PD monitoring pipelines, with direct relevance to remote
patient assessment, drug efficacy trials, and deep brain stimulation
outcome monitoring.
Future directions include paired training with real-world artifact
recordings, downstream evaluation on tremor severity regression, and
lightweight model distillation for deployment on edge devices.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{app:repro}

All experiments use fixed random seed 42 for subject splitting and PyTorch
model initialisation.
The full experimental suite can be reproduced with:
\begin{verbatim}
    python main.py --exp all --epochs 100
\end{verbatim}
Individual experiments can be run selectively, e.g.:
\begin{verbatim}
    python main.py --exp arch   --epochs 100  --tag final
    python main.py --exp latent --epochs 100
    python main.py --exp noise_types --epochs 100
\end{verbatim}
Each run creates \texttt{results/YYYYMMDD\_HHMMSS[\_tag]/} with all outputs
and a \texttt{run\_config.json} recording the full hyperparameter state.
